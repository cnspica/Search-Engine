            <div class="post-text" itemprop="text"> 
             <p>How do I compute the following integral: $$\int \dfrac{x^4+1}{x^3+x^2}\,dx$$</p> 
             <p><strong>My attempt:</strong></p> 
             <p>We can write $$\dfrac{x^4+1}{x^3+x^2} = \dfrac{A}{x^2} + \dfrac{B}{x} + \dfrac{C}{x+1}$$</p> 
             <p>It is easy to find that $A=1$, $B=2$, and $C=-1$.</p> 
             <p>Therefore </p> 
             <p>$$\frac{x^4+1}{x^3+x^2} = \frac{1}{x^2} + \frac{2}{x} - \frac{1}{x+1}$$</p> 
             <p>Therefore: $$\int \frac{x^4+1}{x^3+x^2}\,dx = \int \frac{dx}{x^2} + \int \dfrac{2\,dx}{x} - \int \frac{dx}{x+1} = -\frac{1}{x} +2\log \vert x\vert - \log \vert x+1 \vert + C$$</p> 
             <p><strong>The problem is</strong> I was supposed to find: $$\int \frac{x^4+1}{x^3+x^2}\,dx = \frac{x^2}{2} - x - \frac{1}{2} - \log \vert x \vert + 2 \log \vert x+1 \vert + C$$</p> 
             <p><strong>Where is my mistake?</strong></p>

            <div class="post-text" itemprop="text"> 
             <p>Your $A,B,C$ are wrong. You can't write the expression in that form because the numerator won't have the correct degree.</p> 
             <p>If you combine the terms, you get $$\frac{A(x+1)+Bx(x+1) +Cx^2}{x^3+x^2}$$</p> 
             <p>Note that the numerator is of degree at most $2$.</p> 
             <p>Instead, just perform polynomial division to get a quotient and a remainder term. You can then do what you did, working with the fractional remainder.</p>

       <div id="answer-1784978" class="answer" data-answerid="1784978" itemscope itemtype="http://schema.org/Answer">

          <tr> 
           <td class="votecell"> 
            <div class="vote"> 
             <input type="hidden" name="_id_" value="1784978"> 
             <a class="vote-up-off" title="This answer is useful">up vote</a> 
             <span itemprop="upvoteCount" class="vote-count-post ">1</span>

           <td class="answercell"> 
            <div class="post-text" itemprop="text"> 
             <p>To break a rational polymomial expression into parts, the degree of the numerator <strong>must be</strong> less than the degree of the denominator.</p> 
             <p>This is not the case with $\dfrac{x^4+1}{x^3+x^2}$. Using long division, we find</p> 
             <hr> 
             <p>\begin{array}{rcccccccc} &amp; &amp; x &amp; - &amp; 1 &amp;\\ &amp; &amp; --- &amp; --- &amp; --- &amp; --- &amp; --- &amp; --- &amp; ---\\ x^3 + x^2 &amp; | &amp; x^4 &amp; + &amp; 0x^3 &amp; + &amp; 0x^2 &amp; + &amp; 1 \\ &amp; &amp; --- &amp; --- &amp; --- \\ &amp; &amp; x^4 &amp; + &amp; x^3\\ &amp; &amp; --- &amp; --- &amp; --- &amp; --- &amp; --- \\ &amp; &amp; &amp; &amp; -x^3 &amp; + &amp; 0x^2 \\ &amp; &amp; &amp; &amp; -x^3 &amp; - &amp; x^2 \\ &amp; &amp; &amp; &amp; --- &amp; --- &amp; --- \\ &amp; &amp; &amp; &amp; &amp; &amp; x^2 &amp; + &amp; 1. \\ \end{array}</p> 
             <hr> 
             <p>So $\dfrac{x^4+1}{x^3+x^2} = x - 1 + \dfrac{x^2 + 1}{x^3 + x^2}$.</p> 
             <p>And you need to solve $\dfrac{x^2+1}{x^3+x^2} = \dfrac{A}{x^2} + \dfrac{B}{x} + \dfrac{C}{x+1}$</p> 
             <p>$x^2 + 1 = A(x+1) + Bx(x+1) + Cx^2$</p> 
             <p>Let $x = -1$ and you get</p> 
             <p>$C = 2$</p> 
             <p>Let $C = 2$ and you get</p> 
             <p>$x^2 + 1 = A(x+1) + Bx(x+1) + 2x^2$</p> 
             <p>$-x^2 + 1 = A(x+1) + Bx(x+1)$</p> 
             <p>$1-x = A + Bx$</p> 
             <p>$A = 1$ and $B = -1$.</p> 
             <p>etc</p>
