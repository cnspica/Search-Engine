   <content:encoded>
    &lt;p&gt;In a &lt;a href="http://scifi.blogoverflow.com/2015/11/a-guide-to-the-dc-cinematic-multiverse/"&gt;previous blog post&lt;/a&gt;, we examined the history and future of the various DC Comics cinematic franchises that make up what I (apparently alone) am calling the DC Cinematic Multiverse. But DC isn&amp;#8217;t the only company to have a massive tangle of cinematic universes under its belt. It&amp;#8217;s long-time rival Marvel has been even more prolific when it comes to adapting their material for the large and small screen. Unlike DC, though, Marvel gave up control over much of it&amp;#8217;s catalog during the dark times (the 1996 bankruptcy and subsequent reorganization), resulting it several different studios having access to bits and pieces of the Marvel world. To this day there is still a lot of confusion over who has what rights, who can be in which films, and on-screen with who else, and which films belong to which shared canon.&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p style="font-size: 80%"&gt;&lt;em&gt;Note: As with the DC post, I am mostly ignoring the animated parts of the Marvel multi-verse. There have been 36 (to date) animated shows and about a dozen animated movies.&nbsp; In general, with&nbsp;two&nbsp;notable exceptions, these shows all exist in their own separate universes, with their own separate designations in the Marvel reality catalog, and otherwise play no role in the live-action TV or movie worlds.&lt;/em&gt;&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p&gt;So, as we did with DC, lets see if we can make some sense of of the tangled mess that is the &lt;strong&gt;Marvel Cinematic Multiverse&lt;/strong&gt;. &lt;span id="more-2639"&gt;&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/p&gt; &lt;h2&gt;Reality Numbers&lt;/h2&gt; &lt;p&gt;The idea of having a lot of separate, fragmented versions of their characters is certainly nothing new to Marvel. Their source comics are famous for having thousands of one-off, spin-off, what-if, alternate-timeline and even alternate-universe settings, where variations on their characters exist as distinct people with distinct pasts. Marvel has gone so far as to identify each of these variations with a &amp;#8220;Marvel Reality Number&amp;#8221;. These numbers are occasionally published in the various editions of the&nbsp;&lt;em&gt;Official Handbook of the Marvel Universe&lt;/em&gt;, and a more up-to-date list can be found &lt;a href="http://www.marvunapp.com/master/earthteaz.htm"&gt;here&lt;/a&gt;, curated by one of the &lt;em&gt;Official Handbook&lt;/em&gt;&amp;#8216;s chief editors.&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p&gt;The primary reality number for&nbsp;the printed source material is&nbsp;&lt;strong&gt;Earth-616&lt;/strong&gt;, the mainstream Marvel universe. In general, issues of Marvel&amp;#8217;s comic books take place there unless otherwise specified. A second, more recent print setting is the&nbsp;&lt;strong&gt;Earth-1610&lt;/strong&gt; Ultimate universe, a &amp;#8220;reboot&amp;#8221; of Marvel&amp;#8217;s entirely history which was created&nbsp;in 2000. The latter is notable for being the source of much of the inspiration for the recent popular movies.&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p&gt;Not all Marvel properties have been given a reality number, but a whole, whole, whole lot of them have been. Whenever possible, the official reality numbers are given here, to help identify and distinguish those properties that do or do not exist within the same shared universe.&lt;/p&gt; &lt;h2&gt;The Early Days&lt;/h2&gt; &lt;p style="text-align: center"&gt;&lt;a href="http://scifi.blogoverflow.com/files/2016/04/MCM_EarlyDays.png"&gt;&lt;img class="alignnone size-medium wp-image-2649" src="http://scifi.blogoverflow.com/files/2016/04/MCM_EarlyDays-300x223.png" alt="MCM_EarlyDays" width="300" height="223" /&gt;&lt;/a&gt;&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p&gt;Unlike DC comics, which historically (thanks mostly to Batman and Superman) had a very strong live-action presence in both TV and movies, Marvel&amp;#8217;s big-screen presence prior to the late-1990&amp;#8217;s was very limited. There were a handful of movies and a bunch of television shows produced during that time; while the&nbsp;animated shows were pretty popular, the live-action ones did not fare very well.&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p&gt;The first big-screen adaption of Marvel&amp;#8217;s (actually, at the time, Timely Comic&amp;#8217;s) work came in 1944, with a&nbsp;Captain America film (actually a serial film, broken up into 15 parts) made by Republic Pictures. They had previously adapted other super-hero properties to film, but &lt;em&gt;Captain America&lt;/em&gt; was their last.&nbsp;While the film was generally well received by critics, it was way over budget, and was hardly even a Captain America&nbsp;movie. It contained none of the traditional elements of the Captain America character: he was not a solider, was not named Steve Rogers, didn&amp;#8217;t fight Nazis, and carried a pistol&nbsp;instead of a shield, just for starters. It&amp;#8217;s generally assumed that the movie was written for a different character (likely Fawcett/DC character Mr. Scarlet) but was changed at the last minute. Marvel included a nod to this movie in &lt;em&gt;Captain America: The First Avenger&lt;/em&gt;, in which Steve Rogers stars in a serial film early in his career.&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p&gt;After that, the next time Marvel&amp;#8217;s work appeared on&nbsp;film was in&nbsp;&lt;em&gt;Howard the Duck&nbsp;&lt;/em&gt;in 1986, followed&nbsp;by direct-to-video adaptations&nbsp;&lt;em&gt;The Punisher&lt;/em&gt; and &lt;em&gt;Captain America&lt;/em&gt;. All three of these films were dismal failures, and the disastrous reception of&nbsp;&lt;em&gt;Howard the Duck&lt;/em&gt; would scare film studios away from any theatrical&nbsp;movie based on a Marvel property until after the bankruptcy.&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p&gt;During this same period, Marvel was&nbsp;also dipping its foot into the small-screen pool, trying out a couple of&nbsp;live action TV series based on their characters. Their first attempt was &lt;em&gt;The Amazing Spider-Man&lt;/em&gt; in 1977, which was popular but ultimate cancelled by CBS (who felt they had too much comic-book material on their network). Between 1977 and 2000, about a dozen live-action shows&nbsp;and made-for-TV movies were put on the air, including two really bad X-Men rip-offs, another&nbsp;&lt;em&gt;Captain America &lt;/em&gt;movie, and a &lt;em&gt;Nick Fury&lt;/em&gt; movie starring David Hasselhoff. None of these adaptations was very well received, and they have all been mostly lost into&nbsp;the mists of time.&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p&gt;The shining diamond in among all of this rough is was the highly popular &lt;em&gt;The Incredible Hulk&lt;/em&gt; television franchise. The only genuinely successful franchise in Marvel&amp;#8217;s portfolio prior to &lt;em&gt;Blade&lt;/em&gt;, the show would eventually encompass not only 5 seasons of episodic television, but three made-for-TV movies between 1977 and 1990. Collectively, these make up the Marvel universe&nbsp;&lt;strong&gt;Earth-40005&lt;/strong&gt;&lt;/p&gt; &lt;h2&gt;The Movie License&nbsp;Explosion&lt;/h2&gt; &lt;p&gt;In the mid 1990s, Marvel was experiencing some major financial problems (for a number of reasons), which led to Marvel Entertainment Group filing for bankruptcy. Soon after that, Marvel&amp;#8217;s new CEO decided to take advantage of their vast, untapped vault of characters by bringing them to the big screen. Marvel Entertainment Group formed a new&nbsp;movie production company, Marvel Studios, to turn their comic properties into feature films.&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p&gt;Marvel had previously&nbsp;optioned a few characters to film studios, and had gotten frustrated&nbsp;that none of them ever came to fruition. Most notably, Fox licensed the X-Men characters as early as 1993, and a Spider-Man man movie had been &amp;#8220;in production&amp;#8221; for almost 25 years. In addition, there had been the occasional &amp;#8220;contractually obligated&amp;#8221; movie, such as an unreleased &lt;em&gt;Fantastic Four&lt;/em&gt; movie, that were&nbsp;produced with no intention of ever&nbsp;being seen &amp;#8212; they existed solely to allow the studios to hold onto their properties a bit longer.&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p&gt;Marvel&amp;#8217;s new idea&nbsp;this time around was&nbsp;to sell &amp;#8220;pre-packaged&amp;#8221; movies, with Marvel controlling all of the pre-production and creative aspects, e.g. writing, casting, hiring directors, etc., and handing off to a bigger studio for filming and distribution. Ultimately, Marvel&amp;#8217;s executives didn&amp;#8217;t have quite that much creative control over their movies, but even so, CEO Avi Arad began aggressively&nbsp;working to get Marvel characters into movie theaters. The first movie produced and filmed and distributed under this new system &amp;#8212; and the first in over 10 years &amp;#8212; was &lt;em&gt;Blade&lt;/em&gt;.&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p&gt;Over the course of the 1990&amp;#8217;s, dozens&nbsp;of Marvel&amp;#8217;s character licenses&nbsp;would be&nbsp;acquired&nbsp;by at least six different movie studios. The success of &lt;em&gt;Blade&lt;/em&gt; finally spurred these studios to start converting their own properties into movies, and between 2002 and 2007, 15 Marvel-based movies would be released.&nbsp;Each&nbsp;of these movies exists in their own separate universe &amp;#8212; the idea of a shared movie universe hadn&amp;#8217;t yet cropped up. That idea would have to wait for Marvel to decide to take matters into their own hands.&lt;/p&gt; &lt;h2&gt;The Off-Brands&lt;/h2&gt; &lt;p&gt;While most of Marvel&amp;#8217;s cinematic properties are based on characters from their primary Earth-616 universe, or their alternative Earth-1610 Ultimate universe, there are a few movies that are based on completely unrelated properties. These movies have done pretty well in the theater, but few people associate them with Marvel Comics. These include properties&nbsp;that are&nbsp;published through Marvel&amp;#8217;s Icon Comics imprint, which they use to publish creator-owned series. The list of movies based on Marvel&amp;#8217;s lesser-known properties includes:&lt;/p&gt; &lt;ul&gt; &lt;li&gt;&lt;em&gt;Kick-Ass, Kick-Ass 2&lt;/em&gt; (Icon Comics), licensed by&nbsp;Universal Pictures&lt;/li&gt; &lt;li&gt;&lt;em&gt;Men In Black, Men in Black 2, Men in Black 3&lt;/em&gt; (acquired from Malibu Comics), licensed by Sony/Columbia Pictures&lt;/li&gt; &lt;li&gt;&lt;em&gt;Kingsmen: The Secret Service&lt;/em&gt; (Icon Comics), licensed by Fox&lt;/li&gt; &lt;/ul&gt; &lt;h2&gt;The Cinematic Flops&lt;/h2&gt; &lt;p style="text-align: center"&gt;&lt;a href="http://scifi.blogoverflow.com/files/2016/05/MCM_Licensed_Flops.png"&gt;&lt;img class="alignnone size-medium wp-image-2682" src="http://scifi.blogoverflow.com/files/2016/05/MCM_Licensed_Flops-300x206.png" alt="MCM_Licensed_Flops" width="300" height="206" /&gt;&lt;/a&gt;&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p&gt;The list of Marvel mainstream properties that were farmed out to other studios is pretty long, and contains some of Marvel&amp;#8217;s biggest names. Most&nbsp;of these licensed characters never made it to&nbsp;the big screen, and were eventually abandoned by their licensees. Many of them would later reappear in Marvel&amp;#8217;s own movies or TV shows, but some&nbsp;are just&nbsp;sitting around gathering dust.&nbsp;Of those that did make it into theaters, only a very select few were successful. But first, here are the ones that weren&amp;#8217;t:&lt;/p&gt; &lt;h3&gt;Artisan&lt;/h3&gt; &lt;p&gt;Artisan Pictures (which has since been bought by Lionsgate)&nbsp;received the licensing rights to make a Black Panther movie, but it never materialized. The movie never seemed to have gotten beyond the casting rumor stages. The license eventually expired in 2005, and was one of the first to return to Marvel.&lt;/p&gt; &lt;h3&gt;Lionsgate&lt;/h3&gt; &lt;p&gt;Lionsgate films obtained two properties from Marvel, though only one of them ever made it into theaters. They received a license for Black Widow but let that license expire unused. The other property they got was Punisher, which was made into two films: &lt;em&gt;The Punisher&lt;/em&gt; in 2004, and &lt;em&gt;Punisher: War Zone&lt;/em&gt; in 2008. These are two standalone movies &amp;#8212; the second is a reboot of the first &amp;#8212; and do not share any continuity.&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p&gt;These films are notable for being two of the very few&nbsp;R-Rated movies based on Marvel properties, and the last ones until &lt;em&gt;Deadpool&lt;/em&gt; in 2016. Unfortunately, neither movie was very well-received, and Lionsgate let the license deal expire sometime in 2010, when Marvel announced at SDCC they had gotten the character back. Still, it would be another 6 years before Frank Castle would show up on-screen again.&lt;/p&gt; &lt;h3&gt;Universal Studios&lt;/h3&gt; &lt;p&gt;Universal Studios made a number of licensing deals with Marvel, including for their theme parks, but only one film property: The Incredible Hulk.&nbsp;This was one of the deals that had been lagging for a long time, possibly as far back as 1990, but was kicked into high gear in the 2000-era success of Marvel&amp;#8217;s characters.&nbsp;They brought in famed director Ang Lee, fresh off &lt;em&gt;Crouching Tiger, Hidden Dragon&lt;/em&gt;&nbsp;to convert this into a film, which was released in 2003. The film was rather controversial among Marvel fans, since it changed a lot of the character&amp;#8217;s backstory, but it was commercially pretty successful (not quite making double it&amp;#8217;s budget but coming close). Nonetheless, Universal decided not to follow up on it, and made a deal with&nbsp;Marvel Studios to reacquire the property. Marvel briefly considered making a sequel, before deciding to reboot the character, and to incorporate him into their planned massive movie&nbsp;franchise.&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p&gt;One interesting aspect about this deal is that, while Universal can no longer make Hulk movies, they have retained the right to&nbsp;&lt;em&gt;distribute&lt;/em&gt; any Hulk movies that Marvel does make. Several people at Marvel, including actor Mark Ruffalo (the most recent Bruce Banner) have implied that the Disney-owned Marvel will likely not make another solo Hulk movie as long as the distribution rights rest with competitor Universal, but the Hulk is still free to appear in&nbsp;&lt;em&gt;other&lt;/em&gt; character&amp;#8217;s movies, or the ensemble films.&lt;/p&gt; &lt;h3&gt;Sony/Columbia Pictures&lt;/h3&gt; &lt;p&gt;Sony, via their Columbia Pictures studio, acquired a number of Marvel properties.&nbsp;They were quite successful with one&nbsp;of those (we&amp;#8217;ll get to that), but much less so with the others.&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p&gt;They originally had access to Thor and Luke Cage, but were never able to make much progress on either of them and let them pass back to Marvel. Their final property&nbsp;at least got taken to film: Ghost Rider. The 2007 &lt;em&gt;Ghost Rider&nbsp;&lt;/em&gt;movie was made enough money&nbsp;to warrant a 2012 sequel, &lt;em&gt;Ghost Rider: Spirit of Vengeance,&nbsp;&lt;/em&gt;but both movies were roundly panned by both fans and critics alike. Sony decided to end the franchise at two movies, and let their license expire.&lt;/p&gt; &lt;h3&gt;Fox&lt;/h3&gt; &lt;p&gt;Of all the studios to get access to Marvel&amp;#8217;s licenses, Fox is far and away the most successful at it. Fox is the only one to even come close to matching Marvel Studio&amp;#8217;s success. However,&nbsp;much like Sony, their one massive success is accompanied by some utter failures. While (as far as I know) Fox actually made movies from all of the properties they licensed,&nbsp;not all of them turned out very well.&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p&gt;Fox&amp;#8217;s&nbsp;first stumble was with the 2003 movie &lt;em&gt;Daredevil&lt;/em&gt;, which broke a streak of 6 good Marvel&nbsp;movies in a row dating back to 1998, but somehow warranted an even worse 2005 sequel focusing on Daredevil&amp;#8217;s anti-hero girlfriend &lt;em&gt;Elektra&lt;/em&gt;. Fox thankfully stopped at two here, and let the property fall back into Marvel&amp;#8217;s hands, where they promptly turned it into&nbsp;a phenomenal television series.&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p&gt;Fox&amp;#8217;s other property hasn&amp;#8217;t done much better: a 2005 adaptation of &lt;em&gt;Fantastic Four&nbsp;&lt;/em&gt;wasn&amp;#8217;t bad (certainly not as bad as many other licensed films), but the 2007 follow-up &lt;em&gt;Fantastic Four: Rise of the Silver Surfer&nbsp;&lt;/em&gt;seemed to kill the franchise for a while. Fox tried again with the disastrous 2015 reboot &lt;em&gt;Fant4stic&lt;/em&gt;&nbsp;[sic, ugh], which was one of the worst-received comic book movies in a very long time. However, at least as of mid-2016, Fox has not yet given up on the property, and there is still talk of a possible sequel (though the planned release date was quickly scrapped).&lt;/p&gt; &lt;h2&gt;The Cinematic Marvels&lt;/h2&gt; &lt;p style="text-align: center"&gt;&lt;a href="http://scifi.blogoverflow.com/files/2016/05/MCM_Licensed_Successes.png"&gt;&lt;img class="alignnone size-medium wp-image-2688" src="http://scifi.blogoverflow.com/files/2016/05/MCM_Licensed_Successes-300x223.png" alt="MCM_Licensed_Successes" width="300" height="223" /&gt;&lt;/a&gt;&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p&gt;With that out of the&nbsp;way, lets turn our attention to something much more positive: the resounding success a few studios have had with their Marvel licenses. Overall, there were three distinct&nbsp;Marvel franchises &amp;#8212; each with their own licensee &amp;#8212; that&nbsp;became successful and popular movie adaptations, and brought comic-book movies back into the mainstream.&lt;/p&gt; &lt;h3&gt;Blade&lt;/h3&gt; &lt;p&gt;New Line Cinema has the distinction of producing the first genuinely successful movie franchise based on Marvel&amp;#8217;s property, with it&amp;#8217;s three-film series about Blade, the vampire hunter.&nbsp;The first &lt;em&gt;Blade&lt;/em&gt; film was released in 1998, and was a huge box-office success, spawning not only two sequels, but a short-lived television series set after &lt;em&gt;Blade: Trinity&lt;/em&gt;. The character in the movie was altered slightly from his comic counterpart (he was made into a half-vampire, with the commensurate boosts in strength and speed); after the success of the movie, the Earth-616 Eric Brooks was updated to be more like his &lt;strong&gt;Earth-26320&lt;/strong&gt;&nbsp;counterpart.&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p&gt;The first movie was a big hit with comic book fans, and the sequel fared even better, earning more than it&amp;#8217;s predecessor. Unfortunately, in a pattern not-uncommon in comic book movies, after the second film, things started to go downhill.&nbsp;&lt;em&gt;Blade: Trinity&lt;/em&gt; was roundly panned by critics, and made less money than &lt;em&gt;Blade 2&lt;/em&gt;. At this point, New Line ran out of things to do with the character, and the final movie was also plagued with personnel issues (largely revolving around star Wesley Snipes). New Line tried to branch out into a television series, but was unsuccessful, and subsequently let the license agreement lapse.&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p&gt;&lt;em&gt;Blade: Trinity&lt;/em&gt; is notable for being the first of several comic-book movies starring Ryan Reynolds, who would go on to play DC&amp;#8217;s Green Lantern, as well as two different versions of Marvel&amp;#8217;s Deadpool. The series is also notable for all three films being R-Rated, a relative rarity in the superhero world.&lt;/p&gt; &lt;h3&gt;Spider-Man&lt;/h3&gt; &lt;p&gt;While they had pretty bad luck with most of their licenses, Sony/Columbia did manage to make one of their characters into a popular and&nbsp;successful franchise: Spider-Man.&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p&gt;Sony actually brought out two distinct series of movies for the Spider-Man character. The first,&nbsp;set in &lt;strong&gt;Earth-96283&lt;/strong&gt;,&nbsp;started with &lt;em&gt;Spider-Man&lt;/em&gt; in 2002, and produced two sequels,&nbsp;showcasing a number of classic Spider-Man villains, such as The Green Goblin, Doctor Octopus, and Venom. The movies were initially well received, though there were some key changes in the character&amp;#8217;s backstory. Most notably, Peter&amp;#8217;s first romantic interest is Mary Jane (Gwen Stacy doesn&amp;#8217;t show up until late in the series), and he has organic webbing instead of inventing his own. Unfortunately,&nbsp;each film in the series was slightly less acclaimed (though all of them were commercially successful), and when the director dropped out of the planned fourth movie, Sony already had reboot plans underway to replace it. These films have supplied two of the most infamous scenes in all of comic-book movie history: the classic upside-down kiss from &lt;em&gt;Spider-Man&lt;/em&gt;, and the utterly horrible dancing Peter scene from &lt;em&gt;Spider-Man 3&lt;/em&gt;.&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p&gt;In 2012, Sony released &lt;em&gt;The Amazing Spider-Man&lt;/em&gt;, now set in &lt;strong&gt;Earth-120703&lt;/strong&gt;,&nbsp;which once again told the origin story of the superhero. This version adhered a bit more closely to the comic roots &amp;#8212; Peter invents his webbing and mechanical web slingers, and his love interest is Gwen Stacy. This time, Peter starts off initially facing The Lizard, and the follow-up &lt;em&gt;The Amazing Spider-Man 2&lt;/em&gt; pits him against Green Goblin and Electro. Again, the first movie was generally well-received and a box-office success, but the second one significantly less so. After five attempts at a Spider-Man movie (which included plans for a Sinister Six cross-over that never materialized), Sony was finally willing to let the character go.&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p&gt;In 2015, Marvel and Sony announced that they had finalized a deal to bring Spider-Man into Marvel&amp;#8217;s incredibly&nbsp;phenomenally popular movie franchise. Sony still retains some degree of control over the character, and (similar to Universal and Hulk) will retain distribution rights, but the production aspects of the next Spider-Man movie will include heavy involvement from Marvel, and occur within the shared continuity of Marvel&amp;#8217;s other films.&lt;/p&gt; &lt;h3&gt;The X-Men&lt;/h3&gt; &lt;p&gt;As mentioned, Fox has had, by far, the most success with their Marvel licenses. Indeed, they&amp;#8217;re the only ones who have continued to renew their license deals. Their first big-screen adaptation was the 2000 movie &lt;em&gt;X-Men&lt;/em&gt;, which launched the second most successful Marvel&nbsp;cinematic franchise in history (trailing only slightly behind Marvel&amp;#8217;s own movie franchise.)&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p&gt;The X-Men franchise consists of, at present count, nine movies (including the upcoming&lt;em&gt; X-Men: Apocalypse&lt;/em&gt;), with at least a half-dozen more at various stages of development. While&nbsp;&lt;em&gt;X-Men&lt;/em&gt; director Bryan Singer has largely driven the creative direction of this franchise, several spin-off movies were produced without him.&nbsp;Unfortunately, this has turned the franchise into possibly the most confusing cinematic universe of any licensee, thanks to a variety of continuity glitches. And now, with the added bonus of time-travel, the &lt;strong&gt;The X-Men Cinematic Universe&nbsp;&lt;/strong&gt;consists of two separate and distinct &amp;#8220;realities&amp;#8221;.&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p&gt;The initial series of movies&lt;em&gt;: X-Men&lt;/em&gt;, &lt;em&gt;X-Men: United&lt;/em&gt;, and &lt;em&gt;X-Men: The Last Stand&lt;/em&gt;, form the core of what Marvel has identified as reality number&nbsp;&lt;strong&gt;Earth-10005&lt;/strong&gt;.&lt;strong&gt;&nbsp;&lt;/strong&gt;They subsequently released two prequel&nbsp;movies, &lt;i&gt;X-Men Origins: Wolverine&lt;/i&gt;&nbsp;and&nbsp;&lt;em&gt;X-Men: First&lt;/em&gt; Class, plus a second solo movie, &lt;em&gt;The Wolverine&lt;/em&gt;, set sometime after&nbsp;&lt;em&gt;The Last Stand&lt;/em&gt;. While&nbsp;the&nbsp;solo movie managed to stay pretty consistent with the existing canon (until the very end), the two prequels introduced a lot of problems with continuity, including characters meeting out of order, having unrealistic ages, etc. Fox ultimately&nbsp;attempted to clean up this mess by introducing time travel in &lt;em&gt;X-Men: Days of Future Past&lt;/em&gt;, which didn&amp;#8217;t exactly fix the continuity problems so much as sweep them under the rug. All of those problems still&nbsp;exist in Earth-10005,&nbsp;but that is no longer the &amp;#8220;correct&amp;#8221; reality; all movies from &lt;em&gt;Days of Future Past&lt;/em&gt; forward exist in a new, revised timeline (which Marvel hasn&amp;#8217;t yet assigned a reality number). Singer has explicitly claimed that the events of &lt;em&gt;The Last Stand&lt;/em&gt; and &lt;em&gt;Origins: Wolverine&nbsp;&lt;/em&gt;are no longer canon, though no specific mention was made about the other movies.&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p&gt;In addition to all the X-Men films, Fox has also branched out into other mutant films; the first of these being the solo&nbsp;&lt;em&gt;Deadpool&lt;/em&gt; (notable for being the first R-rated comic movie&nbsp;in 8 years), which is set in the same reality as the current X-Men movies. Fox has also announced plans for a solo Gambit movie, as well as a possible X-Force movie in the future.&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p&gt;Special mention needs to be given to two&nbsp;mutant characters Scarlet Witch and Quicksilver. These characters are mutants, and Magneto&amp;#8217;s children, which makes them very closely tied into the X-Men storylines. However, they&nbsp;are also core members of the Avengers team, who&amp;#8217;s license was retained by Marvel. As such, an explicit exemption was built into the Fox deal allowing Marvel to keep using these two characters, as long as no mention is made of their mutant origin.&nbsp;While Fox has yet to use Wanda, they have used (and continue to use) Pietro, meaning there are now two different versions of Quicksilver in two different cinematic universes.&lt;/p&gt; &lt;h2&gt;The Marvel Cinematic Universe&lt;/h2&gt; &lt;p style="text-align: center"&gt;&lt;a href="http://scifi.blogoverflow.com/files/2016/05/MCM_MCU.png"&gt;&lt;img class="alignnone size-medium wp-image-2700" src="http://scifi.blogoverflow.com/files/2016/05/MCM_MCU-300x253.png" alt="MCM_MCU" width="300" height="253" /&gt;&lt;/a&gt;&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p&gt;In 2004, after seeing the success that other studios were having with their properties, Marvel&amp;#8217;s new COO decided it was time to begin self-financing movies. Their original project was to make&nbsp;10 movies over 8 years, all distributed by Paramount Pictures, but produced and filmed and financed by Marvel themselves. Marvel still had a ton of character properties that were unlicensed, and movies were planned for characters such as Captain America,&nbsp;Ant-Man, Doctor Strange, and Hawkeye. Pretty soon, however, Marvel began reacquiring rights to properties that had been licensed but unused, starting with Black Panther (which Columbia Pictures let lapse in 2005), Iron Man (from&nbsp;New Line Cinema in 2005), Hulk (from Universal&nbsp;in 2006), and Thor (from Sony, also in 2006). These last three would go on to be the first three movies in &lt;strong&gt;Earth-199999: &lt;/strong&gt;the&lt;strong&gt; Marvel Cinematic Universe&lt;/strong&gt;.&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p&gt;What immediately set these movies apart was that the MCU was a&nbsp;&lt;strong&gt;shared universe.&lt;/strong&gt; Characters and events from earlier movies&nbsp;reappeared in later ones, and the ending scene of each movie teased the next film in the series. This all lead up to one of the biggest movie events in superhero history:&nbsp;&lt;em&gt;The Avengers&lt;/em&gt;, which brought together four separate solo movie franchises into a single film. Since then, each new Marvel film has built on this shared story, sometimes in big ways (e.g.&nbsp;&lt;em&gt;Captain America: Civil War&lt;/em&gt;), and sometimes in small ones (e.g.&nbsp;&lt;em&gt;Ant-Man&lt;/em&gt;), but always with the goal of telling one single, epic, galaxy-spanning story.&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p&gt;In 2009, Marvel was acquired by Disney,&nbsp;who took over distribution of the Marvel Studio movies when their Paramount deal ended. Disney subsequently bought back the rights to all the existing Paramount films, leaving &lt;em&gt;The Incredible Hulk&lt;/em&gt; as the only MCU movie not wholly-owned by Disney. At the same time, more of Marvel&amp;#8217;s licensed properties kept reverting to them on a steady basis, including ones that had seen big-screen adaptations already: Blade, Ghost Rider, Daredevil, and Punisher. In 2015, Sony Pictures agreed to a restructuring of their Spider-Man license that effectively reverts control of the character to Marvel, meaning that the only Marvel comics franchises that are not under Marvel Studios control are also unfortunately two of their biggest: the X-Men and the Fantastic Four, both still licensed to Fox.&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p&gt;(As noted earlier, Fox also has license deals for Scarlet Witch and Quicksilver; Marvel introduced the Maximov twins in&nbsp;&lt;em&gt;Age of Ultron&lt;/em&gt;, but killed off Pietro in that film. Thus, at least at this point, Fox has effectively exclusive use of Quicksilver, and Marvel has effectively exclusive use of Scarlet Witch.)&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p&gt;In 2010, Marvel expanded their movie studios to include television production, brought their Marvel Animation production in-house, and began work on a live-action Marvel television show. &lt;em&gt;Marvel&amp;#8217;s Agents of S.H.I.E.L.D&lt;/em&gt;., a spin-off of the &lt;em&gt;Avengers&lt;/em&gt; movie, was the first live-action Marvel show since the short-lived &lt;em&gt;Blade&lt;/em&gt; series in 2006, and with their renewal for a fourth season, is&nbsp;now second only to &lt;em&gt;The Incredible Hulk&lt;/em&gt; as the longest running&nbsp;such show. Marvel also&nbsp;branched out into online streaming with a series of Netflix-based programs, with &lt;em&gt;Daredevil&lt;/em&gt; and &lt;em&gt;Jessica Jones,&lt;/em&gt; plus the upcoming &lt;em&gt;Luke Cage&lt;/em&gt; and &lt;em&gt;Iron Fist&lt;/em&gt;, culminating in&nbsp;a planned crossover show &lt;em&gt;Defenders&lt;/em&gt;. They also produce &lt;em&gt;Powers&lt;/em&gt;, a show exclusive to the Playstation Network.&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p&gt;As of this moment, the Marvel Cinematic universe is, by a very large margin, the most successful film franchise in history; with another dozen films on the books for the next few years and at least one more TV show, it shows no signs of slowing down.&lt;/p&gt; &lt;h2&gt;Animated Shared Universes&lt;/h2&gt; &lt;p&gt;Like the DCCM, Marvel&amp;#8217;s cinematic multiverse has been home to a huge number of animated programs, which I have largely ignored here for space reasons. In general, the majority of those shows are&nbsp;set in their own separate universes. However, there have been two sets of animated TV series that are set in shared universes, and deserve special mention:&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p&gt;&lt;strong&gt;Earth-8096&lt;/strong&gt; is a&nbsp;shared continuity&nbsp;consisting of several animated films and television series that aired between 2009 and 2012, including &lt;em&gt;Hulk Vs. (2009), Thor: Tales of Asgard (2011),&nbsp;Wolverine and the X-Men (2009), &lt;/em&gt;and&nbsp;&lt;em&gt;Avengers: Earth&amp;#8217;s Mightiest Heroes (2010-2012)&lt;/em&gt;. In addition, there were two limited-edition runs&nbsp;of print comics spun off of&nbsp;from the latter program that are set in the same universe. In 2012,&nbsp;&lt;em&gt;Avengers: Earth&amp;#8217;s Mightiest Heroes&lt;/em&gt; was cancelled, thus ending the Earth-8096 story.&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p&gt;&lt;strong&gt;Earth-12041&lt;/strong&gt;, the &amp;#8220;&lt;em&gt;Marvel Animated Universe&lt;/em&gt;&amp;#8220;, is&nbsp;the shared universe where all of Marvel&amp;#8217;s currently active (and a few finished) animated series take place. It began upon the cancellation of the last Earth-8096 show, and the creation of&nbsp;&lt;em&gt;Marvel&amp;#8217;s Avengers Assemble&lt;/em&gt;. The shows in this universe more closely follow the tone and characterizations of the Marvel Cinematic Universe, though the actual plots and storylines are drawn from multiple comics sources.&nbsp;At the moment, the universe includes the animated series&nbsp;&lt;em&gt;Marvel&amp;#8217;s Avengers Assemble (2013-), Hulk and the Agents of S.M.A.S.H (2013-2015), Ultimate Spider-Man (2012-), &lt;/em&gt;and&lt;em&gt; Marvel&amp;#8217;s Guardians of the Galaxy (2015-)&lt;/em&gt;.&nbsp;As with Earth-8096, most of these&nbsp;shows now have spin-off print comic series, also set in the same shared universe&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p&gt;Finally, extra special recognition needs to go to&nbsp;&lt;strong&gt;&lt;em&gt;Big Hero 6&lt;/em&gt;,&lt;/strong&gt; an animated film based off an obscure Japanese superhero team from Earth-616. Not only is this the first (and only, so far) animated Marvel movie to be released into theaters, it&amp;#8217;s also the first Marvel-based film to win an Academy Award (for Best Animated Feature).&lt;/p&gt;

   <content:encoded>
    &lt;p&gt;After watching a few random episodes of &lt;em&gt;Doctor Who&lt;/em&gt; over the previous several years (I saw the 2006 Christmas special live on TV, and then a selection of episodes from Series 4 and 5 around Christmas 2011), I started to get seriously into the franchise around Christmas 2014, and now consider myself a fully fledged Whovian, having watched over two-thirds of all New Who episodes in not much more than a year. Series 9, the most recently released, was the first &lt;em&gt;Doctor Who&lt;/em&gt; I&amp;#8217;ve watched week by week while it was coming out.&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p&gt;The series was largely well received, having been hailed by various critics as &lt;a href="http://chat.stackexchange.com/transcript/30174?m=26054008#26054008"&gt;the best Moffat season yet&lt;/a&gt;, &lt;a href="http://chat.stackexchange.com/transcript/198?m=26046419#26046419"&gt;the best since Eccleston&lt;/a&gt;, or even &lt;a href="http://uk.ign.com/articles/2015/12/12/doctor-who-season-9-review"&gt;the best season of New Who&lt;/a&gt;. Personally I think Series 4 is still my favourite New Who series, but I did very much enjoy this new one, and Capaldi could now give Tennant a run for his money as my favourite Doctor.&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p&gt;This series contained more multi-episode stories than any other for a long time. This allowed more time for each story to develop, mature, and conclude without feeling overly rushed; on the other hand, it detracted from the self-containedness that many of the best &lt;em&gt;Doctor Who&lt;/em&gt; episodes have, and made it harder for casual viewers to &amp;#8216;drop in&amp;#8217; for just a single episode.&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p&gt;Jenna Coleman secured the title of the longest-serving Companion of New Who towards the end of this series before her eventual departure. Peter Capaldi has now really settled into his role as the Doctor, bringing some of his own flair to the show and ensuring his place in the annals of Whovian history. In Series 8 he had to spend too much time acting grumpy and proving that he wasn&amp;#8217;t Matt Smith, but now he&amp;#8217;s been able to spread his wings, let his hair down a bit, and establish himself as (in my view) the best speech-making Doctor in all of New Who. Oh, and speaking of hair, was I really the only one to notice the swap in hair length between the two main characters?&lt;/p&gt; &lt;div id="attachment_2530" style="width: 310px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"&gt;&lt;a href="http://scifi.blogoverflow.com/files/2016/03/DW-series-8-9-bigger.jpg"&gt;&lt;img class="wp-image-2530 size-medium" src="http://scifi.blogoverflow.com/files/2016/03/DW-series-8-9-bigger-300x155.jpg" alt="Hair in series 8-9" width="300" height="155" /&gt;&lt;/a&gt;&lt;p class="wp-caption-text"&gt;Good hair day or bad hair day?&lt;/p&gt;&lt;/div&gt; &lt;p&gt;Now without further ado, let&amp;#8217;s start on the episode-by-episode summaries. Naturally, &lt;strong&gt;SPOILER ALERT&lt;/strong&gt;.&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p&gt;&lt;span id="more-2525"&gt;&lt;/span&gt;&lt;/p&gt; &lt;h1&gt;Episode 1: The Magician&amp;#8217;s Apprentice&lt;/h1&gt; &lt;p&gt;As an episode with historical significance for the show, this has its merits: the &lt;a href="http://scifi.stackexchange.com/questions/103257/have-these-two-doctor-who-characters-ever-met-before"&gt;first-ever episode featuring both Davros and the Master/Missy&lt;/a&gt;, the latter&amp;#8217;s mysterious return to the show, some interesting backstory for the former (I still get a chill when thinking about the final line of the cold open: &amp;#8220;Davros. My name is Davros.&amp;#8221;). As the first episode of a series &amp;#8211; the one which should be a gentle introduction for people who&amp;#8217;ve never seen the show before &amp;#8211; it&amp;#8217;s pretty bad. Anyone who doesn&amp;#8217;t know who Davros is (which includes some regular viewers, if not any true Whovians) wouldn&amp;#8217;t get the point of the pre-credits scene; anyone who&amp;#8217;s unfamiliar with Missy or the Daleks would be lost throughout.&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p&gt;Much of this episode feels like pointless filler just to get an episode&amp;#8217;s worth of material. The whole business of the planes stopping is unrelated to the main story, just a plot device to bring Missy back into the fray. The Doctor&amp;#8217;s dramatic entrance, riding on a tank and playing a guitar, is a typical Moffatism: so &amp;#8220;awesome&amp;#8221; that it&amp;#8217;s right on the edge of crossing into &amp;#8220;ridiculous&amp;#8221;. (And if he&amp;#8217;d said &amp;#8220;dude&amp;#8221; one more time, I would&amp;#8217;ve &lt;em&gt;screamed&lt;/em&gt;.) Eventually the main story kicks off, when the trio &amp;#8211; the trio? hang on, why is Missy even hanging out with our heroes? &amp;#8211; for some reason accept a ride from Davros&amp;#8217;s henchman rather than taking the TARDIS.&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p&gt;By now there&amp;#8217;s not much time left in the episode: just enough for Davros to play some old &lt;em&gt;Doctor Who&lt;/em&gt; clips to the Doctor while Missy and Clara find themselves on &amp;#8211; &lt;em&gt;cue dramatic music&lt;/em&gt; &amp;#8211; Skaro. The cliffhanger ending is so dire that it seems almost ridiculously contrived just in order to have a cliffhanger (that Moffatism again). Clara dead, the TARDIS destroyed, the Doctor possibly a killer &amp;#8230; let&amp;#8217;s hope the next episode manages to resolve all these problems in a reasonably believable way.&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p&gt;&lt;strong&gt;Favourite quote:&lt;/strong&gt; Missy gets all the good lines this time. It&amp;#8217;s a toss-up between:&lt;/p&gt; &lt;ul&gt; &lt;li&gt;&lt;p&gt;&amp;#8220;Try, nano-brain, to rise above the reproductive frenzy of your noisy little food chain, and contemplate friendship. A friendship older than your civilisation, and infinitely more complex.&amp;#8221;&lt;/p&gt;&lt;/li&gt; &lt;li&gt;&lt;p&gt;&amp;#8220;No, wait, hang on a minute. &lt;em&gt;Davros&lt;/em&gt; is your arch-enemy now? I&amp;#8217;ll scratch his eye out.&amp;#8221;&lt;/p&gt;&lt;/li&gt; &lt;/ul&gt; &lt;h1&gt;Episode 2: The Witch&amp;#8217;s Familiar&lt;/h1&gt; &lt;p&gt;What this episode lacks in terms of adequately resolving the last episode&amp;#8217;s cliffhanger (whoops! Missy and Clara not dead after all; whoops! TARDIS easily rematerialised), it makes up for with a good deal of deep conversation between Davros and the Doctor: far more interesting to watch than the cheap quips and banter of the previous episode. The two parallel storylines &amp;#8211; Missy and Clara making their way back in from the desert; Davros and the Doctor sparring together &amp;#8211; mesh together reasonably well.&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p&gt;Missy and Clara&amp;#8217;s initial conversation (complete with shots of the First/Fourth/Twelfth Doctor in an off-screen adventure) gives us an interesting little insight into the Doctor&amp;#8217;s mind, and if you listen carefully it also explains how Missy survived her apparent death at the end of Series 8. Later on we learn something new about how Daleks work, their every action translated by their tank into gunfire and cries of &amp;#8220;Exterminate&amp;#8221;. I&amp;#8217;m pretty sure this contradicts previous canon, but, well, that&amp;#8217;s &lt;em&gt;Doctor Who&lt;/em&gt; for you. And was I the only one to think immediately of the film &lt;em&gt;The Italian Job&lt;/em&gt; when Missy&amp;#8217;s final scene ended?&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p&gt;The episode&amp;#8217;s centrepiece is the conversation between Davros and the Doctor, setting the stage for what I feel is one of the main themes of this series: showcasing Capaldi as perhaps the greatest speech-making Doctor ever. The game of strategy between the two old enemies goes deep as first one then the other gains the upper hand. Finally, just when Davros seems to have secured his victory, the Doctor turns out to have been (as usual) pulling the strings all along. As the city collapses around them, he brings back the TARDIS using &amp;#8230; wait for it &amp;#8230; sonic sunglasses. Ugh. Millions of Whovians just threw their hands up in disgust. We&amp;#8217;ve known and loved the sonic screwdriver for years, and replacing it by some &amp;#8216;wearable technology&amp;#8217; just feels like a betrayal.&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p&gt;&lt;strong&gt;Favourite quote:&lt;/strong&gt;&lt;/p&gt; &lt;ul&gt; &lt;li&gt;&amp;#8220;There&amp;#8217;s no such thing as the Doctor. I&amp;#8217;m just a bloke in a box, telling stories. And I didn&amp;#8217;t come here because I&amp;#8217;m ashamed. A bit of shame never hurt anyone. I came because you&amp;#8217;re sick and you asked. And because sometimes, on a good day, if I try very hard, I&amp;#8217;m not some old Time Lord who ran away. I&amp;#8217;m the Doctor.&amp;#8221;&lt;/li&gt; &lt;/ul&gt; &lt;h1&gt;Episode 3: Under the Lake&lt;/h1&gt; &lt;p&gt;This has all the elements of a classic &amp;#8220;base under siege&amp;#8221; episode, which &lt;em&gt;Doctor Who&lt;/em&gt; has been doing well for decades: a small, clearly defined cast and set; an insidious, poorly understood threat; and no means of escape. As usual there&amp;#8217;s a slightly obnoxious &amp;#8216;money man&amp;#8217; &amp;#8211; Pritchard &amp;#8211; but while such characters often end up surviving just to show it&amp;#8217;s not always the most likeable ones who live (e.g. in &lt;em&gt;Voyage of the Damned&lt;/em&gt; and &lt;em&gt;Silence in the Library&lt;/em&gt; / &lt;em&gt;Forest of the Dead&lt;/em&gt;), this time he&amp;#8217;s one of the first to be killed. The rest of the crew consists of two men and two women, and don&amp;#8217;t worry: the shippers will be satisfied!&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p&gt;In typical Twelfth Doctor fashion, the Doctor at first pooh-poohs the idea of ghosts, saying there&amp;#8217;s no such thing &amp;#8211; apparently forgetting all the dead souls returned that he&amp;#8217;s encountered before, most recently the Cyberghosts in the Series 8 finale. When he finally comes to accept it, his childish enthusiasm is on the edge between amusing and ridiculous. (The &amp;#8220;nice things to say&amp;#8221; record cards that appear in this scene, on the other hand, are simply ridiculous.)&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p&gt;Quickly it becomes clear that the ghosts have some sort of master plan which involves killing again and again to increase their numbers. The Doctor orders a quarantine, effectively sealing them all in, and masterminds the plan to capture the ghosts. For a moment it feels more like fantasy or horror than scifi, with &lt;em&gt;the dark, the sword, the forsaken, the temple&lt;/em&gt; being some kind of words of power. But once they&amp;#8217;re decoded, we&amp;#8217;re back to sci-fi with submarines and spaceships and suspended animation chambers. Cass and the Doctor are in the middle of working out the whole scheme when the base starts to flood, and just as happened in Series 3&amp;#8217;s &amp;#8220;base under siege&amp;#8221; episode &lt;em&gt;42&lt;/em&gt;, the Doctor is cut off from his Companion. The cliffhanger ending arrives along with what appears to be the Doctor&amp;#8217;s ghost.&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p&gt;&lt;strong&gt;Favourite quote:&lt;/strong&gt;&lt;/p&gt; &lt;ul&gt; &lt;li&gt;&amp;#8220;It&amp;#8217;s OK. I understand. You&amp;#8217;re an idiot. [&amp;#8230;] Sorry, why is this man still talking to me?&amp;#8221;&lt;/li&gt; &lt;/ul&gt; &lt;h1&gt;Episode 4: Before the Flood&lt;/h1&gt; &lt;p&gt;For a show that&amp;#8217;s supposed to be all about time travel, &lt;em&gt;Doctor Who&lt;/em&gt; actually has surprisingly few episodes in which time travel plays a role in the plot: this and the series finale being the only such episodes in Series 9. This episode is also unusual in its breaking of the fourth wall. The first scene is the Doctor explaining to us, the viewers, about bootstrap paradoxes: a common concept in time-travel fiction and one which has come up before in &lt;em&gt;Doctor Who&lt;/em&gt;, most famously in the episode &lt;em&gt;Blink&lt;/em&gt;. I was OK with this break with tradition, except for the thrown-in advertisement for &lt;a href="http://www.obrigadopelospeixes.com/wp-content/uploads/2015/10/tumblr_nw1rlbMo0A1ru83j3o8_400-1.gif"&gt;a certain search engine company&lt;/a&gt;. It also inspired this awesome picture (originally sourced from the &lt;a href="http://www.radiotimes.com/news/2015-10-10/doctor-who-what-is-the-bootstrap-paradox"&gt;Radio Times&lt;/a&gt;):&lt;/p&gt; &lt;div id="attachment_2539" style="width: 310px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"&gt;&lt;a href="http://scifi.blogoverflow.com/files/2016/03/Capalthoven.jpg"&gt;&lt;img class="wp-image-2539 size-medium" src="http://scifi.blogoverflow.com/files/2016/03/Capalthoven-300x181.jpg" alt="Pedtwig van Capalthoven" width="300" height="181" /&gt;&lt;/a&gt;&lt;p class="wp-caption-text"&gt;The composer Petdwig van Capalthoven&lt;/p&gt;&lt;/div&gt; &lt;p&gt;Back in 1980, in a Soviet-style town, we finally meet the undertaker for real. Played by the inimitable Paul Kaye (of &lt;em&gt;Game of Thrones&lt;/em&gt; and &lt;em&gt;Jonathan Strange &amp;amp; Mr Norrell&lt;/em&gt; fame), he provides a little much-needed comic relief to this otherwise rather dark episode, before his mysterious and entirely expected death. The plot thickens as the Doctor&amp;#8217;s ghost turns out to be mouthing something different from the others &amp;#8211; our first clue that it&amp;#8217;s not just another ghost. The old theme of the Doctor facing moral criticism from his human companions, much more prevalent in Old Who than it is today, resurfaces here when Bennett accuses him of having allowed O&amp;#8217;Donnell to die.&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p&gt;As might be expected in an episode based around a bootstrap paradox, the narrative is quite hard to make sense of. Does the Doctor actually change the past, or does he not? It took me a while to work out, but it all makes sense provided you&amp;#8217;re OK with certain ideas having no clear origin. The Fisher King &lt;em&gt;did&lt;/em&gt; create the writing to turn Prentis, Moran, Pritchard, and O&amp;#8217;Donnell into ghosts, and the events of the previous episode &lt;em&gt;were&lt;/em&gt; part of its evil scheme just as we thought (it was only the Doctor&amp;#8217;s ghost that wasn&amp;#8217;t), but it was destroyed in the flood and never took its place in the suspended animation chamber. It&amp;#8217;s one of those episodes that you need to spend a while puzzling over after you finish watching, but worth the effort. Oh and just like I promised, all the shippers are satisfied at the end: we have Bennett-O&amp;#8217;Donnell for those who like tragic romances, and Cass-Lunn for those who prefer happy ones.&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p&gt;&lt;strong&gt;Favourite quote:&lt;/strong&gt;&lt;/p&gt; &lt;ul&gt; &lt;li&gt;&amp;#8220;Off the map. Out of the rule book. What if I don&amp;#8217;t die? What if I refuse? I&amp;#8217;m going to go back to the base and I&amp;#8217;m going to save Clara, because that&amp;#8217;s what I do. And I don&amp;#8217;t see anyone here who&amp;#8217;s going to stop me.&amp;#8221;&lt;/li&gt; &lt;/ul&gt; &lt;h1&gt;Episode 5: The Girl Who Died&lt;/h1&gt; &lt;p&gt;Several of this season&amp;#8217;s episodes start with the Doctor and Clara just emerging from, or talking about, their previous (off-screen) adventure. This is in contrast to episodes in the RTD era, which usually started with the TARDIS materialising somewhere and the Doctor and his Companion jumping straight into their latest adventure. The idea is presumably to show just how much time Clara spends with the Doctor &amp;#8211; not just the adventures we see on-screen, but untold dozens in between &amp;#8211; so that the scale of his grief at losing her at the end of the series makes more sense. The actual story here is straightforward: Doctor shows up, human village under threat from alien warriors, all seems lost, Doctor miraculously saves the day using unexpected resources and a strong team effort.&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p&gt;The main purpose of the episode is to introduce an important recurring character for this series: Ashildr. For those who weren&amp;#8217;t following the rumours that Maisie Williams&amp;#8217;s character was going to have a significant role, this was made clear by the Doctor&amp;#8217;s reaction to her in her first few moments on the screen. Although most of her character development comes after this episode, the way we view her changes significantly here too: from the warrior maiden declaring war on the Mire, to the semi-outcast who&amp;#8217;s always been different from the others, to the frightened girl who loves her home and her friends, this is someone the showrunners have put an unusual amount of effort into characterising.&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p&gt;We also get a nice case study of the Doctor. This is when he&amp;#8217;s always at his finest: put him in what seems to be an impossible situation, with what seems to be a bunch of useless people, and watch him use every last scrap of the resources available and force everyone to play to their strengths and bring out their inner talent. After his inevitable victory, the fate of the one-off Companion is taken beyond &amp;#8220;she dies saving the day&amp;#8221; (a la &lt;em&gt;Voyage of the Damned&lt;/em&gt;) to &amp;#8220;she apparently dies and comes back to life&amp;#8221; (a la &lt;em&gt;The Doctor&amp;#8217;s Daughter&lt;/em&gt;), and even a step beyond that since her coming back to life is an issue in itself: it makes her a new kind of creature, an anomaly like Jack Harkness. And as part of what spurs the Doctor to save her, we get something we&amp;#8217;ve never had before: an explanation for where he got one of his faces. Another moment with historical significance for the show as a whole.&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p&gt;&lt;strong&gt;Favourite quote:&lt;/strong&gt;&lt;/p&gt; &lt;ul&gt; &lt;li&gt;&amp;#8220;No, no, not Vikings! I&amp;#8217;m not in the mood for Vikings!&amp;#8221; &lt;em&gt;(It doesn&amp;#8217;t sound very funny here, but the tone and facial expression with it made me crack up.)&lt;/em&gt;&lt;/li&gt; &lt;/ul&gt; &lt;h1&gt;Episode 6: The Woman Who Lived&lt;/h1&gt; &lt;p&gt;This is what they call a Companion-lite episode; Clara is basically absent throughout, except for a short scene at the very end. It&amp;#8217;s also, despite what some of the internet will tell you, almost completely separate from the previous episode. The issue of whether or not they&amp;#8217;re officially considered a two-parter is &lt;a href="http://movies.stackexchange.com/q/50655/27759"&gt;complicated&lt;/a&gt;, but the stories being told are entirely different and linked only by a single character, who also appears in other episodes towards the end of the series. Once again, the main purpose is to explore Ashildr&amp;#8217;s character, and the actual story is even less interesting than the previous episode&amp;#8217;s: attempted invasion of Earth by big cat aliens, thwarted at the last minute by using their own technology against them.&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p&gt;It&amp;#8217;s obvious straight away that Ashildr (I&amp;#8217;m going to keep on calling her that) has greatly changed since we last saw her. No longer going by the same name, no longer seeming to care about the village where she grew up, this callous and aloof woman is a far cry from the innocent girl we met last time. And it&amp;#8217;s clear almost from the start that she&amp;#8217;s not telling the Doctor everything, which heightens our mistrust for her. She&amp;#8217;s a fascinating study in what immortality &amp;#8211; never growing old, losing everyone you love, but always continuing on &amp;#8211; can do to a person. As she herself points out, she&amp;#8217;s similar to the Doctor in this respect; the difference between the two lies in how they deal with the problems of an extended lifespan. The exploring of powerful moral issues like this, which lead us to respect the Doctor on a deep level, are one of the things I love most about the show.&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p&gt;Sam Swift initially seems to be nothing more than comic relief (bad comic relief at that, with some quite terrible jokes), but he ends up at the very centre of events. As with several one-off &lt;em&gt;Doctor Who&lt;/em&gt; characters, the showrunners leave the option open for him to appear again, by not resolving the question of whether or not he&amp;#8217;s as immortal as Ashildr after being killed and brought back to life. Ashildr, on the other hand, establishes herself as a recurring presence in the Doctor&amp;#8217;s life and leaves us almost certain that she&amp;#8217;ll turn up again, and in a way more significant than simply a background figure in photos of Clara. As indeed she will &amp;#8230;&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p&gt;&lt;strong&gt;Favourite quote:&lt;/strong&gt;&lt;/p&gt; &lt;ul&gt; &lt;li&gt;&amp;#8220;Ashildr! It did a good job of setting the tone for the rest of the Netflix shows, something followed up by and capitalised on by the significantly better &lt;i&gt;Jessica Jones&lt;/i&gt;.&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p&gt;Although the second season of &lt;i&gt;Daredevil&lt;/i&gt; fails to reach the heights set by &lt;i&gt;Jessica Jones&lt;/i&gt;, it&amp;#8217;s still an improvement over the first season in nearly every way &amp;#8211; primarily thanks to the way that show-runners Doug Petrie and Marco Ramirez seem to have listened to and acted on criticisms of the first season. The most obvious strength that the second season has over the first is also the most simple &amp;#8211; the second season of &lt;i&gt;Daredevil&lt;/i&gt; has enough going on to actually justify it&amp;#8217;s length.&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p&gt;There are two fairly distinct stories at play in the second season of &lt;i&gt;Daredevil&lt;/i&gt;. Whereas Elektra is just another side character for Matt to interact with (and not a particularly well-developed one at that, despite the best efforts of actress Elodie Yung), Frank acts as an entirely separate viewpoint into the season, more like a secondary protagonist than anything else.&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p&gt;And it works incredibly well &amp;#8211; Frank actually ends up being the most interesting character that the show has produced so far thanks to some excellent writing that really dives into the psychology of the character, a handful of truly brutal action scenes and a phenomenal performance from Jon Bernthal. This is the definitive version of The Punisher, and I&amp;#8217;d watch a show centered around him in a heartbeat &amp;#8211; but I have to admit that I&amp;#8217;d rather see him co-star in future seasons of &lt;i&gt;Daredevil&lt;/i&gt;, simply because his uneasy relationship with Daredevil and the ideological differences between them help make Matt less boring.&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p&gt;Because I think we need to admit that Matt was a little boring in the first season. And although that&amp;#8217;s still somewhat true here, the Matt we meet in the second season is simple more confident in himself, and over the course of the season we grow to realise one very important thing about him &amp;#8211; he absolutely loves beating people up, a character flaw that bites him in the ass towards the latter half of the season but also makes him significantly more fun to be around. Take the incredibly impressive stairwell fight sequence towards the end of the third episode for example &amp;#8211; a little laugh to himself just before the fighting starts tells us everything we need to know about who Matt Murdoch really is.&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p&gt;Also more interesting this season are both Karen and Foggy, who spent most of last season trying to solve a mystery that both we the audience and Matt himself already knew the answer to. &lt;i&gt;Daredevil&lt;/i&gt; makes no such mistake this time around &amp;#8211; other than one instance where Karen finds out some information just minutes before it is all revealed anyway, they both have their own stories independent of Matt&amp;#8217;s adventures, the resulting being that they both really develop as characters over the course of the season. For Foggy, that means learning that he has value outside of his relationship to Matt (which is pretty meta considering some of the reactions that people had to him in the first season), and although Karen&amp;#8217;s is a little harder to define without entering the realm of incredibly vague spoilers, I will say that I enjoyed the way that it used one of the more controversial aspects of the first season to its advantage.&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p&gt;The only real flaws that &lt;i&gt;Daredevil&lt;/i&gt;&amp;#8216;s second season suffer from are those relating to the way that various storylines conclude. The overall ending to the big mystery of the season doesn&amp;#8217;t really make much sense, and the season finale hinges around being invested in a huge fight sequence against an organisation whose unclear goals mean that they never really seem all that threatening &amp;#8211; but even then &lt;i&gt;Daredevil&lt;/i&gt; kind of makes it work, the execution of each conclusion being good enough to get away with the fact that it doesn&amp;#8217;t quite feel right and could have done with a rewrite or two. And that says it all really &amp;#8211; by the time the season finale came around, I was so invested in what was going on that I was willing to deal with a substandard conclusion simply because of how good that which came before had been.&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p&gt;There just isn&amp;#8217;t an area where the second season of &lt;i&gt;Daredevil&lt;/i&gt;&nbsp;doesn&amp;#8217;t improve on the first, and if the almost inevitable third season can continue that trajectory we could be looking at something really special. The action is better, the stories more engaging, the characters more interesting &amp;#8211; hell, we even get more time with Nelson and Murdock actually being lawyers, another example of the show seemingly listening to criticism and trying to act on it. So I&amp;#8217;ll say just this in my attempt to influence the third season of the show &amp;#8211; can we please get a proper visualisation of Matt&amp;#8217;s radar sense?&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p&gt;Oh, and fix the costume already.&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p&gt;&lt;i&gt;This article was originally published on &lt;a href="http://screennerds.blogspot.com/" target="_blank"&gt;ScreenNerds&lt;/a&gt;.&lt;/i&gt;&lt;/p&gt;

I&amp;#8217;m pretty sure sound only carries over a medium (like water, or air) and not through the empty vacuum of space. One online review said this submarine nonsense was a homage to &lt;em&gt;Das Boot&lt;/em&gt;, because JÃ¼rgen Prochnow was in that movie doing the exact same thing. Blair goes back to Taggart and asks him why everyone hates Pilgrims. Turns out Taggart is also a Pilgrim! Taggart says the Pilgrims thought they were better than everyone else and the universe hates them for it. Sounds legit.&lt;/p&gt; &lt;div id="attachment_2456" style="width: 675px" class="wp-caption aligncenter"&gt;&lt;a href="http://scifi.blogoverflow.com/files/2016/02/wingcommander.2.jpg"&gt;&lt;img src="http://scifi.blogoverflow.com/files/2016/02/wingcommander.2.jpg" alt="Maybe they hate him for being a Pilgrim. The Diligent (remember it?) is apparently still around and undamaged, so they load all the pilots into space suits to launch a boarding party against the Comm ship. I guess pilots make great marines. As they leave the Tiger Claw is attacked by some kind of teleporting torpedo, and Deveraux abandons the mission of &lt;em&gt;saving Earth&lt;/em&gt; to stop it. Her ship is subsequently ruined and she is left in a pod drifting in space. A fitting end for a species traitor like her.&lt;/p&gt; &lt;p&gt;Blair makes the jump to Earth and sends the intel, but he was followed! Remember that black hole thing at the beginning of the movie? Now the Kilrathi fleet starts showing up one ship at a time and the Terran fleet mops them up like fish in a space barrel. The Tiger Claw shows up and Deveraux was saved (Boo!).
