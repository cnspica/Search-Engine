            <div class="post-text" itemprop="text"> 
             <p>I'm new to Stack&nbsp;Overflow and haven't any reputation points. I just got my first question answered, and I tried to upvote the answer, but I get a popup that says I don't have enough reputation points yet.</p> 
             <p>I can understand the basic principle - give people incentives to "give back" to the community, so I don't have a problem with restricted privileges at first.</p> 
             <p>But <em>it's my question</em>! Who's in a better position to judge if it's a good answer or not?</p> 
             <p>(Note: One of the answers to the Meta Stack Overflow question <em><a href="http://meta.stackexchange.com/questions/509/why-dont-people-upvote-questions-they-answer">Why don’t people upvote questions they answer</a></em> implies I should have been able to upvote in this instance, but I can't. Change in policy? Bug in the code?) </p>

           <td class="answercell"> 
            <div class="post-text" itemprop="text"> 
             <p>As a rep-less newbie, you've not yet earned the site's trust. Fortunately, it's not hard: ask a good question or post a good answer, and you'll quickly have that ability - all it takes is two up-votes!</p> 
             <p>As the author of a question, the site does grant you one special ability: you can mark an answer as "accepted", proclaiming to the world that it helped you solve your problem. IMHO, this is actually quite a bit more helpful than merely being able to up-vote: once a question has an Accepted answer, passing readers are able to read that answer knowing that it actually worked, and thereby better understand your problem. Not to mention the little bit of satisfaction it can give to the kind author of that answer...</p> 
             <p>So be patient, new user! You'll be able to up-vote soon enough. Until then, exercise the abilities that you do have to their full extent!</p>

             <p>So once you get enough reputation points, upvote the answer you wanted to before. It's that simple :)</p>

           <td class="answercell"> 
            <div class="post-text" itemprop="text"> 
             <p>Even a new person can reward a good answer by "accepting" it. That increases the answerer's reputation by 15 points, more than the ten points awarded for an upvoted answer. So you've had your chance to "pass judgment."</p> 
             <p>Just as important, accepting an answer gives the questioner two points. If nothing else happened to you on the site, it would take seven questions and acceptances to bring your reputation from 1 to 15, thereby allowing you to upvote answers.</p> 
             <p>As a practical matter, if you're asking good questions and accepting good answers, one or two questions should get you the three (question) upvotes you need to get to 15. This could also be achieved by two upvotes and two acceptances. </p> 
             <p>And if you aren't asking good questions (or providing good answers), the site doesn't want you to vote. The 15 rep is a minimal (but not zero) hurdle to achieve this result.</p>

           <td class="votecell"> 
            <div class="vote">

             <a class="vote-up-off" title="This answer is useful">up vote</a> 
             <span itemprop="upvoteCount" class="vote-count-post ">-1</span>

           <td class="answercell"> 
            <div class="post-text" itemprop="text"> 
             <p><em>This is a straightforward and obvious change that should be implemented.</em> </p> 
             <hr> 
             <p>I don't agree with <strong>previous arguments for this policy</strong>: </p> 
             <ul> 
              <li><p>At <a href="http://meta.stackexchange.com/q/41347/187282">Why are 15 reputation points required to upvote?</a>, it is argued that the threshold is needed to prevent people from creating new accounts to upvote themselves. However, this would be mitigated by only allowing new users to vote on answers to their own question. </p></li> 
              <li><p>At <a href="http://meta.stackexchange.com/q/88560/187282">Allow upvote for &lt;15 rep users on own questions</a>, Jeff argued that allowing new users / askers to only accept (for +15 reputation), but not upvote (for +10 reputation), prevents someone from gaming the system for more reputation by asking questions under a different account, answering from their primary account, and upvote themselves from the new account, over and over. This is a bizzare, exaggerated (IMHO), and shortsighted argument. Someone could create a second account, ask questions, answer from their primary account, and <em>accept</em> their own answer from the new account, over and over, without really any greater difficulty. (Although this is blatantly obvious, and I thought of it independently, I should disclose that this idea was mentioned already in a comment <a href="http://meta.stackexchange.com/questions/38834/allow-new-users-to-vote-on-answers-theyve-received/38843#comment751681_38843">here</a> by @jmoreno, and <a href="http://meta.stackexchange.com/questions/1661/shouldnt-a-reputation-points-less-newbie-at-least-be-able-to-upvote-a-good-answ#comment2592_1661">above</a> by @KyleCronin.) </p></li> 
             </ul> 
             <p>On the other hand, there are <strong>potential issues with the status quo</strong>: </p> 
             <ul> 
              <li><p>In a <a href="http://meta.stackexchange.com/questions/1661/shouldnt-a-reputation-points-less-newbie-at-least-be-able-to-upvote-a-good-answ#comment2594_1661">comment</a> at the top of this thread, @IanElliott notes: </p> 
               <blockquote> 
                <p>I'll be honest, I typically avoid answering question from people with 1 rep because usually they come from google or some other source looking for a quick answer. Once it's provided they leave and don't check back even though a correct answer has been provided. (Out of personal experience) </p> 
               </blockquote></li> 
              <li><p>Furthermore, a number of people have stated, here and elsewhere, that they tend to upvote new users just to get them past the threshold so they can vote. This strikes me as an unintended effect of the policy, and one that (while well meaning) somewhat undermines other aspects of the SE system. </p></li> 
             </ul> 
             <p>(I am not criticizing them, I am somewhat sympathetic to both of these inclinations, for what it's worth.) However, these tendencies would be preempted if people could vote on their own threads. </p> 
             <p>I am likewise unpersuaded by <strong>the contention that new users have other options and the hurdle is low anyway</strong>: </p> 
             <ul> 
              <li><p>Yes, a new user can accept, but may have gotten useful help from more than one answer. The OP may also have gotten useful help from an answer (and want to upvote it), but may not believe the answer fully resolved their issue (and so not want to accept it). </p></li> 
              <li><p>Many have argued here (and on related meta.SE threads) that it is 'easy' to get 3 upvotes for your question. This does not seem so obvious to me. An asker may only have one question, get the information they need, and not continue with SE to build reputation. Additionally, my impression is that many sites, and in particular SO, have low levels of (up)voting. For example, <a href="http://stackoverflow.com/search?q=is%3Aquestion+score%3A0+answers%3A2">this search</a> suggests that (as of May 12th, 2016) there are 1,675,568 questions to which more than 1 answer has been provided without a single upvote. Moreover, in 1,058,925 of those threads (&gt; 63%), the OP accepted an answer. (There are &gt; 45k with 5+ answers, no upvotes, and an accepted answer, and 1 open question with 17 answers meeting those criteria.) </p></li> 
             </ul>
