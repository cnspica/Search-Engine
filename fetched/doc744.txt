            <div class="post-text" itemprop="text"> 
             <p>I and my colleague have a difference of opinion. </p> 
             <p>We are creating a chart of, lets say, fruits. Like this one: </p> 
             <p><a href="http://i.stack.imgur.com/XpDWH.jpg"><img src="http://i.stack.imgur.com/XpDWH.jpg" alt="enter image description here"></a></p> 
             <p>But what if the value of oranges is 0%? Do we show the legend anyway? Like this:</p> 
             <p><a href="http://i.stack.imgur.com/8gckq.jpg"><img src="http://i.stack.imgur.com/8gckq.jpg" alt="enter image description here"></a></p> 
             <p>Or do we leave it out? Like this: </p> 
             <p><a href="http://i.stack.imgur.com/gxshQ.jpg"><img src="http://i.stack.imgur.com/gxshQ.jpg" alt="enter image description here"></a></p> 
             <p>We have a dashboard with a bunch of these kind of charts. One of us wants to keep the legend of oranges to keep the design consistent (all charts have three values), and the other one wants to keep it clean and only show the information that is truly important. All charts show the same three fruits, but with different values of course. </p>

           <td class="answercell"> 
            <div class="post-text" itemprop="text"> 
             <h3>Show the numbers.</h3> 
             <p>If the user selected three fruits to be shown, Apples, Oranges, and Melons, and only sees Apples and Melons in the pie and in the legend, they will wonder where the Oranges went. If the Oranges <em>are</em> shown in the legend but not in the pie, again they will look for the tiny slice of Orange with a magnifying glass.</p> 
             <p>Therefore, show the numbers, confirming to the user that, yes, they <em>did</em> select Oranges as well, but there just aren't any.</p> 
             <p>A way of doing this would be "disabling" the Oranges in the legend (greying them out) and appending "(none)", "(not shown)", or something similar.</p>

             <p>Definitely keep it in the legend.</p> 
             <p>If you only have the two items in the legend it looks like you created a chart to show the comparison of Apples and Melons. For all the user knows there could be twice as many Oranges and you simply didn't include them in this data. Since Oranges <em>are</em> included you need to show that. </p> 
             <p>A second reason would be if the user manually selects these categories to compare. If the user selects "Show me Oranges, Melons, and Apples" and you return a chart with just Melons and Apples they are going to assume the tool is broken. </p> 
             <p>A good middle ground would be to include Oranges in the legend but give it a "disabled" or lower opacity style, to imply that there aren't any.</p>

            <div class="post-text" itemprop="text"> 
             <p><strong>Don't use a pie chart</strong></p> 
             <p>Your problem stems from the fact you're using a pie chart. Pie charts are simply not a very good way of representing data (see <a href="https://www.stevefenton.co.uk/2009/04/pie-charts-are-bad/">here</a>, <a href="http://www.businessinsider.com/pie-charts-are-the-worst-2013-6?IR=T">here</a>, <a href="https://blogs.oracle.com/experience/entry/countdown_of_top_10_reasons_to_never_ever_use_a_pie_chart">here</a> or <a href="https://www.geckoboard.com/blog/pie-charts/#.VzSOc3oa7qE">here</a> for some discussions of why) and you've stumbled on one of the reasons why: they can't represent data with a value of 0. They're also almost impossible to accurately read the values off which is why you so often see them annotated with the actual values - this alone should tell you they're a bad form of data representation, good representations don't require you to redundantly repeat yourself.</p> 
             <p><strong>Instead use a bar chart</strong></p> 
             <p>I spent two seconds knocking these up on Excel but they illustrate the point - your data is now easy to read even with zero values and your user can accurately read off more or less exact values for each of the states:</p> 
             <p><a href="http://i.stack.imgur.com/HB933.png"><img src="http://i.stack.imgur.com/HB933.png" alt="Excel example bar chart"></a></p>

             <p>The answer here depends on how the charts are being used.</p> 
             <p>If the charts are historically factual (the data that they represent is fixed and will not change over time), extra legend entries for zero values are just noise that gets in the way of the users understanding of the chart.</p> 
             <p>However, if the charts are constantly changing, you need to signal to your users that the items that currently have zero values <em>are</em> being monitored too and may appear on the chart. The most understandable way to do this would be to include those items in the legend.</p>

           <td class="answercell"> 
            <div class="post-text" itemprop="text"> 
             <p>We've already established that the person who asked the question wants a pie chart, so the answer should be focused on how to represent the information and values in that chart (whether it should be used or not is another consideration altogether). So in addressing that point, you could look at two things:</p> 
             <ul> 
              <li>Adding labels where appropriate (this could be on the legend or somewhere else)</li> 
              <li>Adding values where appropriate (this could be on the chart or somewhere else)</li> 
             </ul> 
             <p>There are a large number of Microsoft Excel defaults that illustrates this:</p> 
             <p><a href="http://i.stack.imgur.com/v5m2d.png" rel="nofollow"><img src="http://i.stack.imgur.com/v5m2d.png" alt="enter image description here"></a></p> 
             <p>So why not simply test it out with people and see which one they think will be the clearest and easier to understand?</p>
