            <div class="post-text" itemprop="text"> 
             <p>Please note that I've read this <a href="http://math.stackexchange.com/questions/1308992/why-doesnt-a-taylor-series-converge-always">question</a> and it did not address mine. </p> 
             <p>I've been presented with the following argument regarding Taylor series:</p> 
             <blockquote> 
              <p>We have a function $f(x)$, now assume that there exists a power series that's equal to it:</p> 
              <p>$$f(x)=a_0 + a_1 x + a_2 x^2 +\dots$$</p> 
              <p>One can quickly show, using differentiation, that</p> 
              <p>$$f(x) =f(0) + f'(0) x + \dfrac{f''(0)}{2! }x^2 +\dots$$</p> 
             </blockquote> 
             <p>It seems that this argument implies two things:</p> 
             <blockquote> 
              <ol> 
               <li><p>For the Taylor series around a point to exist, it has to be continuously differentiable (infinitely many times) and defined at that point.</p></li> 
               <li><p>If the Taylor series for a function exists, then this implies it's equal to it, or that it converges to the original function at every point $x$.</p></li> 
              </ol> 
             </blockquote> 
             <p>Now I know very well that point 2 is false (not every smooth function is analytic). </p> 
             <p>But point 2 seems to be implied from the argument I presented above which assumes that if a power series exists such that it's equal to the function or in other words, converges to the function for all $x$, then it will be given by the Taylor series. So what's wrong regarding this argument above? </p>

           <td class="answercell"> 
            <div class="post-text" itemprop="text"> 
             <p>You are confusing the following two conditions:</p> 
             <ol> 
              <li><p>The function $f$ admits a Taylor series at (argument) $0$, and</p></li> 
              <li><p>There exists a power series $\sum_ia_ix^i$ that converges to $f(x)$ for all $x$ (in the domain of $f$).</p></li> 
             </ol> 
             <p>While the second condition implies the first (and the Taylor series will then be that $\sum_ia_ix^i$), the first statement does not imply the second. It only means that $f$ is smooth (indefinitely differentiable) in the neighbourhood of$~0$ (namely: its repeated derivatives at$~0$ will define the Taylor series). This is what the first enumerated point in your question seems to acknowledge. But you cannot get from there to the second statement, and the fact that the second implies the first is not helpful in doing so.</p> 
             <p>The <em>only</em> relation required between a function and its Taylor series is that those derivatives and the coefficients of the series match. For a given value $x\neq0$, the Taylor series may or may not converge, but even if it does, this does not (necessarily) give you $f(x)$. To see why not, it suffices to add to $f$ some nonzero function with zero Taylor series; your parenthesised remark seems to indicate you are aware that such functions exist (if not, consider $\exp(-x^{-2})$ extended by continuity at $x=0$). But I repeat, the Taylor series does not have to converge at all, for any $x$ (except for $x=0$).</p> 
             <p>A Taylor series is just a formal power series, a way to collect the information of all derivatives of$~f$ evaluated at$~0$. Indeed (though it takes some work to see this) <em>every</em> formal power series occurs as the Taylor series of some function (in fact of infinitely many of them).</p>

            <div class="post-text" itemprop="text"> 
             <p>Assume that $f$ is infinitely differentiable in a neighborhood of $0$. Then Taylor's theorem about $$f(x)=T_n(x)+R_n(x)\tag{1}$$ says that for <strong>fixed</strong> $n\geq0$ and $x\to0$ you have $$\lim_{x\to0}{R_n(x)\over|x|^n}=0\ .$$ Your question about the Taylor <em>series</em> $T_\infty(x)$ starts with $(1)$ as well, but it refers to the limit $n\to\infty$ for <strong>fixed</strong> $x$ instead. You want to know under which circumstances $$\lim_{n\to\infty} T_n(x)=f(x)\tag{2}$$ for all $x$ in a suitable neighborhood $U$ of $0$. Now $(2)$ is equivalent with $\lim_{n\to\infty}R_n(x)=0$ for each $x\in U$. The latter can only be proven by analyzing the error terms $R_n(x)$, using the various analytical forms available for $R_n(x)$ and, e.g., concrete estimates of the higher derivatives $f^{(n+1)}(x)$ for $x\in U$.</p>

             </blockquote> 
             <p>The big assumption made here is that your function is the sum of a power series.</p> 
             <p>A power series is a series under the form $$\sum_{n=0}^{\infty} a_n x^n $$ Obviously, this series converges for $x=0$.</p> 
             <p>If it doesn't converge <em>only</em> for $x=0$, then there exists a number $R &gt;0$ such as the series converges for all $\vert x \vert &lt; R$, and diverges for all $\vert x \vert &gt; R$. For all $\vert x \vert &lt; R$, you can define the sum of the series as a function:</p> 
             <p>$$\forall x \in ]-R,R[, \quad f(x) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} a_n x^n $$</p> 
             <p>It is then very simple to show that this function is undefinitely differentiable, and that is it equal to its taylor series, i.e. $a_n = \frac{f^{(n}(0)}{n!} $ for all integer $n$.</p> 
             <p>This explain the result stated in your text.</p> 
             <p>PS: the number $R$ is called the <em>radius of convergence</em> of the power series.</p>
