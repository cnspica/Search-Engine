            <div class="post-text" itemprop="text"> 
             <p>Is it a bad idea to post a photo of your keyboard to social media?</p> 
             <p>Can I look at a photo of a keyboard and determine the password of an account?</p> 
             <p>Assuming a certain (set of) password(s) is the most commonly typed character sequence on a given keyboard:</p> 
             <p>Is the resolution in the photo of that keyboard sufficient to determine the most frequently used set of keys, by analyzing the grease patterns on them?</p> 
             <p>If I know the most frequently used keys, is a brute force attack now feasible, since I can limit the size of the dictionary?</p> 
             <p>(Question inspired by when I saw a door protected by a numeric keypad, where the paint was missing on three of the keys. The room number was three digits, none of which had paint on their respective keys. Unsurprisingly, the combination was the room number reversed.)</p>

           <td class="answercell"> 
            <div class="post-text" itemprop="text"> 
             <p>In some cases yes, you can guess the most frequently used keys by the wear marks. That's how I know that apparently I use a lot of the L, M, N, A and E keys - the keys are now just black, the letter is cancelled.</p> 
             <p>But most people don't use the keyboard for their passwords only, and the wear pattern is also influenced by the stroke direction, angle and pressure - keys farther down the keyboard will be pressed differently from those nearer. The keys I wear more appear to be exactly under the hand that controls them, and now that I noticed, I hit them harder than the rest (OK, also I'm a horrible typer).</p> 
             <p>The keyboard might hint (weakly, at what) at that the most frequent typed word, <em>or anagrams thereof</em>, might be. But that's not the same as your <em>password</em> except in very specific cases.</p> 
             <p>So my opinion would be that keyboard pictures are mostly harmless.</p> 
             <p>On the other hand, I sometimes see Post-It with letters and numbers on them attached to monitors and on woodboards behind selfied people, so I'd also say that it's always a very good idea to <strong>review</strong> the photos (as well as <em>whatever else</em>) you post to the social media, looking at the goods with an attacker's eye:</p> 
             <ul> 
              <li>inside view of broken locks and/or brand names useful to research approaches</li> 
              <li>hints about location (this might be relevant to determine whether you're holidaying abroad and estimate how long your home will be vacant)</li> 
              <li>valuable items (might give people ideas either about them, or about your wealth, which also could give people ideas)</li> 
              <li>discrepancies between what's shown and what you told anybody who might get to know, such as employer, insurance company, significant other(s), etc.</li> 
             </ul>

            <div class="post-text" itemprop="text"> 
             <p>You need to clarify the term "keyboard". If you take a pic of my keyboard I am writing this text. You will surely notice some patterns and missing letters from my typing. But be aware that most of the time such keyboards are not used to enter passwords at all. Typically you rather get a heat map of the frequently used chars for a certain natural language. E.g. my <kbd>a</kbd>, <kbd>c</kbd>, <kbd>e</kbd> and <kbd>n</kbd> are no longer visible.</p> 
             <p>I thought about my passwords. When I use natural language to form complex words in it, I of course tend to use the most common chars for my language. When I use or am forced to use special chars, etc. they get hardly used in other scenarios.</p> 
             <p>Now since you are talking about security keyboards, whose purpose is entering a password the answer is "maybe". If many people use one single password all over, then of course the patterns will be visible over time and it should be possible to create permutations of the used digits or chars. How ever, if many people use different passwords on this keyboard you will again only see the patterns of the frequently used digits or chars.</p> 
             <p>This will reduce the search space, but may be not enough to actually get the password as there may be other means of protection, e.g. locking the lock after n failed attempts.</p>

            <div class="post-text" itemprop="text"> 
             <p>Keyboards wise, I'd consider this a minor concern unless I was using the same password for everything. If you're using a different password for everything and changing them reasonably regularly, you're probably fine. Add this to the fact that if you're using the keypad for other things you're going to get a much different wear pattern than just <code>P</code> <code>A</code> <code>S</code> <code>W</code> <code>O</code> <code>R</code> <code>D</code> being worn, you've probably not much to worry about. </p> 
             <p>If like me you're an avid FPS player, (and unlike me you've got one password for everything) by the time wear is visible on the password keys, your WSAD keys are going to be worn as well, you're probably ok.</p> 
             <p>That said, change your passwords regularly and use a random generator to create <a href="https://xkcd.com/936/" rel="nofollow">memorable random passwords</a>. The <a href="http://world.std.com/~reinhold/diceware.html" rel="nofollow">Dice PassPhrase Generator</a> is a good place to start. With a bit of excel wizardry you can create your own password generator based off these principals and presto - memorable random passwords.</p>
