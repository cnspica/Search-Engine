            <div class="post-text" itemprop="text"> 
             <p>Right now at my university we are busy with a project for designing a conceptual aircraft. Our aircraft is in the CS-23 range, this airplane is a small cargo aircraft with the option for carrying injured passengers (so like an ambulance aircraft).</p> 
             <p>Our aircraft will have a cruise hight at 10,000 ft. We want to make this aircraft pressurized but our question is therefor, is it possible to pressurize the aircraft from inside below the normal 8,000ft. So for example, we are flying at 10,000 ft but we want to have the inside pressure having the same as on sea level if that is possible, or is it only possible to pressurize the inside at 8000ft.</p> 
             <p>Thank you in advance.</p>

                 <span class="comment-copy">@Simon: If the cabin is hermetically sealed to maintain the pressure when the door was closed, two very bad things will happen: You'll run out of oxygen in a matter of hours, and the humidity from exhaled breaths will fog up all the windows and begin to condense on all the instruments. Pressurized airplanes continually bring in fresh air and cycle out the air already in the cabin. The airplane is not literally sealed tight.        <div id="answer-27517" class="answer" data-answerid="27517" itemscope itemtype="http://schema.org/Answer"> 
        <table> 
         <tbody>
          <tr> 
           <td class="votecell"> 
            <div class="vote"> 
             <input type="hidden" name="_id_" value="27517"> 
             <a class="vote-up-off" title="This answer is useful">up vote</a> 
             <span itemprop="upvoteCount" class="vote-count-post ">2</span> 
             <a class="vote-down-off" title="This answer is not useful">down vote</a>

           <td class="answercell"> 
            <div class="post-text" itemprop="text"> 
             <p>If you design the aircraft, shouldn't you be answering that question? If it is part of your aircraft's mission to fly at 10.000ft whilst maintaining sea level cabin pressure, then you should design it that way.</p> 
             <p>Why would it only be possible to pressurize the inside to 8000ft? Not to 9000ft, 7000 ft or any other arbitrary altitude?</p> 
             <p>Of course the construction of the aircraft needs to be able to withstand the forces caused by pressure difference between the inside of the cabin and the outside. And the pressurization system needs to be able to maintain the pressure at sea level, so compensating for any air leaks in the pressure vessel. </p> 
             <p>For an initial feasibility study you could look at the pressure difference on the hull when an aircraft is flying at FL430 whilst maintaining cabin pressure at 8.000ft. Note thate the Boeing 787 maintains the cabin altitude at <a href="http://aviation.stackexchange.com/q/1728/19">6.000ft, while operating at 43.000 ft</a>. </p> 
             <p>Compare that to the pressure difference between 10.000ft and sea level. Looking at those pressure differences should give you a feeling whether or not it is easy to achieve your objective. </p>
