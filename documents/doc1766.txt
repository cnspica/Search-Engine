       <h2 class="blog-title"><a href="http://scifi.blogoverflow.com/2016/05/a-guide-to-the-marvel-cinematic-multiverse/">A Guide to the Marvel Cinematic Multiverse</a></h2> 
       <div class="meta-info">
        <span class="postedDate">2016-05-12</span> by 
        <a href="http://scifi.blogoverflow.com/author/MichaelEdenfield/" title="Posts by Michael Edenfield" rel="author">Michael Edenfield</a>. 
        <a href="http://scifi.blogoverflow.com/2016/05/a-guide-to-the-marvel-cinematic-multiverse/#comments">1 comments</a>
       </div> 
       <div class="excerpt_main"> 
        <div class="post-text"> 
         <p>In a <a href="http://scifi.blogoverflow.com/2015/11/a-guide-to-the-dc-cinematic-multiverse/">previous blog post</a>, we examined the history and future of the various DC Comics cinematic franchises that make up what I (apparently alone) am calling the DC Cinematic Multiverse. But DC isn’t the only company to have a massive tangle of cinematic universes under its belt. It’s long-time rival Marvel has been even more prolific when it comes to adapting their material for the large and small screen. Unlike DC, though, Marvel gave up control over much of it’s catalog during the dark times (the 1996 bankruptcy and subsequent reorganization), resulting it several different studios having access to bits and pieces of the Marvel world. To this day there is still a lot of confusion over who has what rights, who can be in which films, and on-screen with who else, and which films belong to which shared canon.</p> 
         <p style="font-size: 80%"><em>Note: As with the DC post, I am mostly ignoring the animated parts of the Marvel multi-verse. There have been 36 (to date) animated shows and about a dozen animated movies.&nbsp; In general, with&nbsp;two&nbsp;notable exceptions, these shows all exist in their own separate universes, with their own separate designations in the Marvel reality catalog, and otherwise play no role in the live-action TV or movie worlds.</em></p> 
         <p>So, as we did with DC, lets see if we can make some sense of of the tangled mess that is the <strong>Marvel Cinematic Multiverse</strong>. <a href="http://scifi.blogoverflow.com/2016/05/a-guide-to-the-marvel-cinematic-multiverse/#more-2639" class="more-link">more » </a></p>

       <h2 class="blog-title"><a href="http://scifi.blogoverflow.com/2016/04/the-great-science-fictionfantasy-tv-showdown/">The Great Science-Fiction/Fantasy TV Showdown!</a></h2> 
       <div class="meta-info">
        <span class="postedDate">2016-04-25</span> by 
        <a href="http://scifi.blogoverflow.com/author/rogue-jedi/" title="Posts by Rogue Jedi" rel="author">Rogue Jedi</a>. 
        <a href="http://scifi.blogoverflow.com/2016/04/the-great-science-fictionfantasy-tv-showdown/#comments">4 comments</a>
       </div> 
       <div class="excerpt_main"> 
        <div class="post-text"> 
         <p><a href="http://scifi.blogoverflow.com/files/2016/04/HungerGames.jpg"><img class="  wp-image-2612 aligncenter" src="http://scifi.blogoverflow.com/files/2016/04/HungerGames-300x208.jpg" alt="HungerGames" width="343" height="238"></a></p> 
         <p>&nbsp;</p> 
         <p>If there’s one thing science-fiction and fantasy fans like more than watching, reading and playing science-fiction and fantasy, it’s <em>arguing</em> about watching, reading and playing science-fiction and fantasy. Marvel vs. DC. <em>Star Wars</em> vs. <em>Star Trek</em>. Classic Who vs. NuWho. These debates may very well rage on for the rest of time.</p> 
         <p>But now, The Science Fiction and Fantasy Stack Exchange is going to what they can to help settle these debates. Every month or so (hopefully), we’ll host a bracket, where all members of the site will be able to vote in order to determine the best of the science-fiction and fantasy best.</p> 
         <p>This month, we’re doing science fiction and fantasy TV shows. I, with help from other members of site, have selected 48 science fiction and fantasy TV shows from across the decades. These 48 shows were chosen by popularity and notability, and range the full spectrum of sub-genres and fan-bases. I have arranged them to hopefully prevent any round one steam-rolling.</p> 
         <p>So without further ado, arranged in no particular order for the primary bracket, the shows!</p>

          <li><p>The Family That Slays Together: <em>Buffy the Vampire Slayer /</em> <em> Angel</em></p></li> 
          <li><p>Who Are You? Who, Who, Who, Who?: Classic Doctor Who / Nu-Who</p></li> 
          <li><p>The Element Of Surprise: <em>Avatar: The Last Airbender /</em> <em> The Legend of Korra</em></p></li> 
          <li><p>Mysteries And Stuff: <em> LOST /</em> <em> X-Files</em></p></li> 
          <li><p>Batman V Batman: Dawn of Batman: <em>Batman</em> (‘60s) / <em>Batman: The Animated Series</em></p></li> 
          <li><p>What A Twist!: <em>The Twilight Zone</em> (Original) / <em>The Outer Limits</em> (Original)</p></li> 
          <li><p>Jedi Did Not See That Coming: <em>Star Wars: The Clone Wars /</em> <em> Star Wars: Rebels</em></p></li> 
          <li><p>Turtle Power!: <em>TMNT</em> (Original) &nbsp;/ <em>TMNT</em> (2012)</p></li> 
          <li><p>No Spoilers!: <em>Game of Thrones /</em> <em> The Walking Dead</em></p></li> 
          <li><p>Space And Stuff: <em>Battlestar Galactica (2004) /</em> <em> Babylon 5</em></p></li> 
          <li><p>DC on CW: <em>Arrow /</em> <em> The Flash</em> (2014)</p></li> 
          <li><p>This IS A Place For A Hero: <em>The Spectacular Spider-Man /</em> <em> Justice League Unlimited</em></p></li> 
          <li><p>Aussies and Brits: <em>Farscape /</em> <em> Red Dwarf</em></p></li> 
          <li><p>Simply Marvelous: <em>Marvel’s Agents of S.H.I.E.L.D /</em> <em> Daredevil</em></p></li> 
          <li><p>Currently Airing Fantasy: <em>Supernatural /</em> <em> Once Upon a Time</em></p></li> 
          <li><p>The Spin-Offs: <em>Stargate: SG-1 /</em> <em> The Sarah Connor Chronicles</em></p></li> 
          <li><p>The Classics: <em>The Incredible Hulk</em> (1978) / <em>The Hitchhiker’s Guide to the Galaxy</em> (TV series)</p></li>

         <p><em>For the record. I hate writing reviews. Why? Because criticism is easy. Any idiot can sit in a theater and have an opinion. Any art critic can, with the weight of their degree in art literacy, define why a thing fails to deliver whatever THEY think it should deliver to an “audience.” As a writer who struggles to find that proper beat to put a story to while I am writing it, I am always reminded movies are a special breed of writing.</em></p> 
         <p><em>Movies are a collaboration. They are a story written by at least one person, massaged by two or three other people, edited by another person before being turned into a script which gets changed daily. As the shots are reviewed and then taken from different angles, all that work is gathered together and digitally enhanced, cinematic effects added, CGI constructs placed and then this beast is edited down to fit a designated time pattern.</em></p> 
         <p><em>This is one of the most important parts of a movie’s creation: No matter what vision a director might have, arbitrary decisions made as to what stays in and what goes out are often made by executives whose reasons for doing what they do are completely their own but presumably were meant to make the film more “profitable,” based on some movie-making algorithm only they understand.</em></p>

         <p>After watching a few random episodes of <em>Doctor Who</em> over the previous several years (I saw the 2006 Christmas special live on TV, and then a selection of episodes from Series 4 and 5 around Christmas 2011), I started to get seriously into the franchise around Christmas 2014, and now consider myself a fully fledged Whovian, having watched over two-thirds of all New Who episodes in not much more than a year. Series 9, the most recently released, was the first <em>Doctor Who</em> I’ve watched week by week while it was coming out.</p> 
         <p>The series was largely well received, having been hailed by various critics as <a href="http://chat.stackexchange.com/transcript/30174?m=26054008#26054008">the best Moffat season yet</a>, <a href="http://chat.stackexchange.com/transcript/198?m=26046419#26046419">the best since Eccleston</a>, or even <a href="http://uk.ign.com/articles/2015/12/12/doctor-who-season-9-review">the best season of New Who</a>. This allowed more time for each story to develop, mature, and conclude without feeling overly rushed; on the other hand, it detracted from the self-containedness that many of the best <em>Doctor Who</em> episodes have, and made it harder for casual viewers to ‘drop in’ for just a single episode.</p>

Peter Capaldi has now really settled into his role as the Doctor, bringing some of his own flair to the show and ensuring his place in the annals of Whovian history. In Series 8 he had to spend too much time acting grumpy and proving that he wasn’t Matt Smith, but now he’s been able to spread his wings, let his hair down a bit, and establish himself as (in my view) the best speech-making Doctor in all of New Who. Oh, and speaking of hair, was I really the only one to notice the swap in hair length between the two main characters?</p>
